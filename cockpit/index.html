Mirror aim

0° = pointed straight down your own flank (you see your car). The driving-school "just see the edge of your car" lands around 0–6°.

The BGE idea: wide enough that your own car is out of the glass and the mirror picks up exactly where the rear-view ends. On this page's flat-glass cones the seamless relay lands near 19°; real convex glass and the lean-your-head ritual land at bigger numbers.

The base pace — but drivers differ: some blitz past, and some camp beside your rear quarter for seconds before committing. Watch what the campers do to the invisible-time metric.

Each new car picks a side at random with this bias. Some start in your lane and merge out before passing — watch them cross the mirrors mid-lane-change.

What to watch

  • Wide aim, one full pass: the car appears in the middle of the rear-view; drifts toward its outer edge; while it's in the rear-view's last quarter it's already entering the side mirror's inner quarter; it sweeps across the side mirror; and as it leaves the glass it's already in the corner of your window, then your windshield. Every hand-off overlaps — it is never in nothing. Same relay, mirrored, on the right.
  • School aim: the side mirror just repeats the rear-view (same patch of road), so both lose the car at the same moment — and it vanishes, still behind your shoulder, until it suddenly fills the side glass. The campers are the scary ones: a driver who sits at your quarter for five seconds is five seconds of nothing.
  • When a car goes invisible, glance toward it. Your neck stops at 60°, so at school aim you catch it only at the very end of the blind zone — the wide mirror had it the whole time. The metrics ignore the glance on purpose: the page measures what you know facing forward.
  • The dash screen runs the top-down view from demo 04, so you can watch where each car really is while your mirrors lie (or don't).
  • Your own paint: with school aim, the inner edge of each side mirror is your own rear door — glass pointed at something bolted to the car.

Same vision geometry as The Mirrors — 16° flat side mirrors, 30° rear-view, ±70° head-still eyes (SAE BGE, Platzer 1995), so the blind-zone numbers agree. The cabin is honest cartoonery: pillars are drawn but not modeled as blockers (a small head-move defeats a pillar; no head-move fixes bad mirror aim), turning your head changes the picture but never the coverage numbers (head-still is the point), and the view is a flat pinhole projection — no lens, no curvature, right-side mirrors on real cars are convex and show more.
