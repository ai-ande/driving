Mirror aim

0° = pointed straight down your own flank (you see your car). The driving-school "just see the edge of your car" lands around 0–6°.

The BGE method: lean your head to the window, set the mirror to just lose your car, sit back up — you land near 25–30°. (It's across the cabin — glance right to see it, like in a real car.)

How fast the passing car slides through your glass. Slow passes camp in the blind zone the longest.

What to watch

  • School aim: the passer leaves your side mirror while it's still behind your shoulder. For the next few seconds it is in nothing — not the rear-view, not the mirror, not your window — and then it's suddenly filling the side glass, already beside you.
  • Wide aim: it never blinks out. Rear-view hands to side mirror hands to your own window, like a relay.
  • The dash screen runs the top-down view from demo 04, so you can watch where the car really is while your mirrors lie (or don't).
  • Your own paint: with school aim, the inner edge of each side mirror is your own rear door — glass pointed at something bolted to the car.
  • When the car goes invisible, glance left (drag the road): your eyes find it instantly. That's the shoulder check — and the metrics ignore it on purpose, because the page measures what you know while facing forward.

Same vision geometry as The Mirrors — 16° flat side mirrors, 30° rear-view, ±70° head-still eyes (SAE BGE, Platzer 1995), so the blind-zone numbers agree. The cabin is honest cartoonery: pillars are drawn but not modeled as blockers (a small head-move defeats a pillar; no head-move fixes bad mirror aim), turning your head changes the picture but never the coverage numbers (head-still is the point), and the view is a flat pinhole projection — no lens, no curvature, right-side mirrors on real cars are convex and show more.
