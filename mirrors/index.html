The Mirrors

The blind spot isn't a fact of physics — it's a setting. If a mirror shows your own car, it's wasting glass on something that never moves. Aim them wide and a passing car is always in something.

Mirror aim

0° = pointed straight down your own flank (you see your car). The driving-school "just see the edge of your car" lands around 0–6°.

The BGE method: lean your head to the window, set the mirror to just lose your car, sit back up — you land near 25–30°.

How fast the passing car slides through your zones. Slow passes camp in the blind zone the longest.

What to watch

  • Driving-school setting: the passing car turns red beside your rear quarter — it's in nothing. That's where lane-change crashes live.
  • Wide setting: rear-view hands off to the side mirror, which hands off to your own eyes. The coverage bar below the road becomes an unbroken rainbow.
  • The test in your real car: can you see your own car in the side mirror? If yes, you own a blind spot you don't need.

Pure geometry — mirror fields of view ~16° (flat glass), rear-view ~30°, forward vision ±80°. The wide setting is the SAE-published BGE method (Platzer 1995), long championed by Car and Driver and Click & Clack. See evidence.
