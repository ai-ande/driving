# The driving thesis

*The source beliefs behind this project, organized. These are claims to be tested — see
[demos.md](demos.md) for how each one gets turned into a provable, playable experiment and
[evidence.md](evidence.md) for how each claim relates to existing research.*

## The claim

A few simple, teachable changes to how individuals drive would dramatically reduce traffic,
increase both safety **and** speed, and lower stress — without new lanes, new roads, or new
technology. Taken to the limit: if every car were driven this way (by trained people or by an
AI that understood these things), congestion as we know it would largely disappear, even on
today's busiest roads.

## Why nobody can see it

Driving is something most adults have done for decades, on autopilot, with simple controls
and seemingly straightforward rules. That familiarity fuses it to the ego:

- Most people believe they are better-than-average drivers — statistically impossible, and
  famously confirmed by research (see evidence.md).
- Because "I am a good driver" is part of identity, any suggestion that one could drive
  differently is processed as a personal attack, triggering defensiveness or rage.
- So people conclude the *system* or *other people* are the problem. Traffic and accidents
  get filed under "inevitable," to be alternately accepted and raged at.
- The benchmark for "good driver" is set so low that essentially everyone clears it. Against
  the standard described here, essentially everyone — including the confident — is untrained.

This isn't just a driving observation; driving is a mirror for how ego blocks improvement
everywhere. When everyone believes they have nothing to learn, everyone's quality of life
quietly degrades, and the anger gets pointed outward (honking matches, bumper stickers,
"what's the big deal, my speed is the correct speed").

## The core misunderstanding

**Your driving does not end with you.** Every unnecessary brake tap, slow reaction, or badly
timed lane change propagates backward through hundreds of cars behind you — and forward in
time, to drivers who haven't even left their driveways yet. "Rush hour traffic" is not a
fixed weather condition; it is the accumulated sum of millions of small, avoidable
disturbances. People optimize their immediate ten meters and are blind to the wave they're
creating.

## Seven misconceptions

| # | What people believe | The counter-belief |
|---|---------------------|--------------------|
| 1 | **Adding lanes fixes traffic.** | Traffic comes from three sources (below), none solved by lanes. Widening often adds traffic — certainly during years of construction, and usually after. |
| 2 | **Blind spots are inevitable.** | They're a mirror-adjustment error. People are taught to aim side mirrors so they can barely see their own car — guaranteeing overlap with the center mirror and a blind zone. Aimed wide, a passing car hands off continuously: center mirror → center+side → side → side+peripheral → peripheral → window. No blind spot, no shoulder check panic. |
| 3 | **A turn signal makes a lane change safe.** | As commonly used, signals *create* conflict: the signaler believes the blinker grants a right to the space; the occupant believes they own it and defends it; the "polite" driver brakes to let them in and launches a slowdown wave behind them. The skilled move: know the whole picture from correct mirrors, wait for real room, change lanes while **accelerating** (never braking), and track what's happening two lanes over so a simultaneous merge into the same lane is anticipated and done diagonally-safe rather than "almost hit them." Signals are for genuine turns — telling traffic you'll do something *unexpected* — not a license to displace people. |
| 4 | **Traffic is an evenly spread mass.** | Traffic travels in globs with a front and a back, often seeded by large trucks. Ahead of every glob is open road until the next glob's tail. It behaves like a wave and could be *synchronized* — people could be taught to flow, spread out, and absorb waves instead of amplifying them. |
| 5 | **Driving means constantly doing things — brake, gas, steer.** | Skilled driving is mostly *not* acting. The brake is a sledgehammer; the accelerator is a fine-resolution dial that also slows the car (lift off → engine drag decelerates you gently, without brake lights, without scaring anyone behind you). Feathering the accelerator and balancing the space ahead means a brake tap is, almost by definition, an admission of a planning mistake. (Proof of concept: downtown San Diego to UTC at 9 a.m. in stop-and-go traffic, routinely with zero to two brake presses total.) |
| 6 | **Traffic is inevitable.** | Traffic is largely *manufactured* by the disturbances above. Remove the disturbances and the same road carries far more cars at far higher speed. |
| 7 | **City driving means stopping at light after light.** | The system wastes most of its capacity at signals: (a) drivers take seconds to react at green and start in a slow ripple instead of together, so half the green is spent on human latency; (b) cities time signals so arterials stop constantly — big roads should almost never stop, side roads should wait longer, some stops should be yields (e.g., dedicated right-turn lanes onto on-ramps); (c) paired one-way streets beat bidirectional arterials (Austin's own 5th/6th couplet is the pattern). |

## The three sources of traffic

1. **Merging** — done inefficiently and *indignantly*, with morality ("I'm being polite" /
   "he's cutting the line") substituting for physics. Entrances, exits, lane drops,
   construction.
2. **Anything that stops or slows cars** — red lights, crashes, rubbernecking at anything on
   the shoulder, and above all **brake lights themselves**, which reproduce backward.
3. **Large vehicles and the packs they create** — people subconsciously cluster (it feels
   safer; it is the opposite: nowhere is more dangerous than beside a car you can't control).
   Trucks intensify this: drivers fear passing them, a pack forms behind, and the whole pack
   inherits the truck's speed — worse when trucks occupy left lanes.

## The teachable skills (first draft)

1. **Mirrors wide** — eliminate the blind zone; know everything behind you at all times.
2. **The accelerator is also the brake** — lift early, glide, keep rolling; treat every brake
   press as a mistake to learn from.
3. **Balance the space ahead** — manage the gap like a resource; absorb waves instead of
   passing them backward amplified.
4. **Move on green like you mean it** — be ready, start with the car ahead (or with the
   light), not seconds after.
5. **Change lanes with speed, into real room** — never force, never brake in, know two lanes
   over, signal what's genuinely unexpected.
6. **Don't pack** — position yourself in open pockets; pass trucks decisively; never cruise
   beside anyone.

## Status

These are beliefs held with high conviction and long personal testing, now being converted
into interactive, falsifiable demonstrations — starting with the signal/reaction simulator
on real Austin streets in this repo. Where a belief conflicts with law or standard guidance
(notably #3 on signaling — most US states require signaling lane changes), the demos will
test the *underlying mechanics* (gap management, speed-matched merges) so the physics can be
evaluated separately from the legal question.
