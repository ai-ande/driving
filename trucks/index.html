Trucks & Packs

A three-lane loop, a few slow trucks, and one human instinct: staying near other cars feels safe. It isn't — nowhere is more dangerous than beside someone you can't control — and it's how packs are born.

The road

Trucks are governed to 56 mph and accelerate slowly. They are not the villain — the packing behavior around them is.

Many states ban trucks from the left lane. Toggle off to allow the classic "elephant race."

Trained: pass decisively when it genuinely pays, hold lane and cushion when the road is dense (churn seeds packs), never cruise alongside anyone.

How the humans drive

Contentment with sitting in a clump: the higher this is, the less often a held-up driver bothers to pass at all.

Demands a much bigger gap before overtaking a truck — or refuses entirely and settles in behind. Everyone behind inherits the truck's speed.

What to watch

  • Toggle trucks keep right off: elephant races nearly double the boxed-in count — the clearest effect in this demo.
  • Crank vehicles past ~110: no driving style saves a road that's simply over capacity (same lesson as the Ring).
  • The honest surprise: in this model, calm lane-holding beats eager passing at density — "doing less" wins again.

Open question — this is the lab's least-settled demo. IDM + gap-acceptance lane changes produce only mild packs (8–16 vehicles), not the giant rolling globs the thesis describes. Either real packs need mechanisms we haven't modeled yet (defensive closing against cut-ins, rubbernecking, speed variance) — or this traffic source is smaller than believed. The lab exists to find out.
