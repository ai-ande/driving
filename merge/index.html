The Merge

A two-lane highway and an on-ramp. Nobody is rude, nobody crashes — yet how people think about merging decides whether the highway keeps flowing or breaks into waves.

How mergers behave

Low: crawl down the ramp and hope. High: use the whole ramp to hit traffic speed, then slide into a gap. Speed difference is what makes merges dangerous and disruptive.

Low: wait for a huge, "safe-feeling" hole (and block the ramp behind you). High: take any gap that physics actually supports.

How the mainline responds

The "polite" move: brake to open a hole for the merger. Feels kind — launches a wave that hits drivers two minutes behind you who never see why.

The trained move: if the left lane is free, change lanes early — room appears without anyone slowing down.

What to watch

  • Today: mergers arrive slow, mainline drivers courtesy-brake, and red fans grow upstream from the ramp — drivers a kilometer back stop without ever knowing why.
  • Trained: mergers arrive at traffic speed and take real gaps; the mainline makes room by moving over, not by braking. Same demand — watch all three numbers.
  • Merging is traffic source #1 in the thesis: most "highway traffic" is born at ramps and lane drops.
